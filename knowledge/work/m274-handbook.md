# M-274 Handbook for Employers — USCIS Reference
## Last Reviewed: April 2025 | Source: USCIS M-274 (Official)

---

## 1.0 Why Employers Must Verify Employment Eligibility

All U.S. employers must complete Form I-9 (Employment Eligibility Verification) for every person hired for employment in the United States. This applies regardless of the person's citizenship or immigration status.

The Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986 (IRCA) requires employers to:
- Verify the identity and employment authorization of all persons hired after November 6, 1986.
- Complete and retain Form I-9 for each employee.
- Not discriminate against individuals in the I-9 verification process based on citizenship, immigration status, or national origin.

Employers who knowingly hire or continue to employ unauthorized workers, or who fail to properly complete and retain I-9 forms, face civil and/or criminal penalties. USCIS, Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), and the Department of Justice (DOJ) enforce I-9 requirements.

**Key legal framework:**
- INA § 274A — establishes employer sanctions for hiring unauthorized workers.
- 8 CFR Part 274a — regulations governing I-9 requirements.
- INA § 274B — anti-discrimination provisions administered by the DOJ Office of Special Counsel (OSC).

## 2.0 Who Must Complete Form I-9

### Employees Required
Every new employee hired for pay or other remuneration after November 6, 1986, working in the United States must complete Form I-9. This includes:
- U.S. citizens and nationals
- Lawful permanent residents
- Aliens authorized to work

### Employees Exempt
Form I-9 is NOT required for:
- Persons hired before November 7, 1986 (and continuously employed)
- Persons employed for casual domestic service in a private home (intermittent or sporadic basis)
- Independent contractors (though employers should not knowingly use contractors who are unauthorized — see Section 12.0 for agricultural contract labor)
- Persons not physically working in the United States

### Employer Definition
"Employer" includes any person or entity that employs one or more individuals for wages or other remuneration. This includes:
- Staffing agencies (considered employers of the workers they place)
- Recruiters who pay workers
- Federal, state, and local government agencies
- Nonprofits and religious organizations

## 3.0 Completing Section 1 — Employee Information and Attestation

### When Section 1 Must Be Completed
The employee must complete Section 1 **no later than the first day of employment** (the actual first day they begin work for pay). Section 1 must NOT be completed before the employee accepts a job offer.

### Who Completes Section 1
The employee completes Section 1. An employer may not complete Section 1 on behalf of an employee. If the employee needs assistance (language barrier, disability), a preparer or translator may help, but the employee must sign and attest.

### Section 1 Content
The employee must provide:
- Full legal name (last, first, middle initial)
- Other last names used (maiden name, aliases)
- Current address (street, city, state, ZIP)
- Date of birth
- U.S. Social Security Number (SSN) — required if employer uses E-Verify; otherwise voluntary unless the employer requires it
- Email address and telephone number (optional)

**Employee attestation — one of four boxes:**
1. A citizen of the United States
2. A noncitizen national of the United States
3. A lawful permanent resident (Alien Registration Number/USCIS Number required)
4. An alien authorized to work until [expiration date] (Alien Registration Number/USCIS Number OR Form I-94 Admission Number OR Foreign Passport Number + country of issuance required)

Employees who select box 4 must provide the expiration date of their employment authorization. If the authorization has no expiration date (e.g., refugees, asylees, certain TPS holders), the employee may write "N/A."

### Preparer/Translator Certification
If someone other than the employee helped complete Section 1, that preparer or translator must complete the certification block: their full name, address, and signature. Multiple preparers each complete a separate certification on the Supplement A page.

## 4.0 Completing Section 2 — Employer Review and Verification

### When Section 2 Must Be Completed
Employers must complete Section 2 **within 3 business days of the employee's first day of employment**. For employees hired for 3 business days or fewer, Section 2 must be completed on the first day of employment.

### Who Completes Section 2
The employer (or authorized representative) completes Section 2 after physically examining original documents presented by the employee. The employer may designate an authorized representative to complete Section 2 on their behalf, but the employer remains responsible for any errors.

### Remote Verification (Authorized Alternative Procedure)
Employers enrolled in E-Verify may use an authorized alternative procedure for remote document examination:
- Employee transmits a clear copy of the document(s) (front and back where applicable).
- Employer examines the copy for authenticity.
- Employer must retain a copy of the document(s) presented (required when using this procedure).
- Employer notes "Alternative Procedure" in the Additional Information field of Section 2.
Employers must apply this alternative consistently — they cannot offer it only to certain employees or in a discriminatory manner.

### Document Examination
The employee presents documents from List A, or one document from List B AND one from List C (see Section 13.0). The employer must:
- Physically examine the original document(s) (or transmitted copy under authorized alternative procedure).
- Determine if the document(s) reasonably appear to be genuine and to relate to the employee.
- NOT request specific documents — the employee chooses which acceptable documents to present.
- NOT reject valid documents that appear genuine.

### Section 2 Content
For each document examined, the employer records:
- Document title
- Issuing authority
- Document number
- Expiration date (if any)
- Employee's first day of employment

The employer (or authorized representative) must sign and date the attestation.

### Special Situations

**Employees Who Are Unable to Produce Documents Within 3 Business Days:**
If an employee cannot produce documents within 3 business days due to loss, theft, or other legitimate reason, the employer may accept a receipt for a replacement document. The employee must present the actual document within 90 days.

**Receipts Accepted as Temporary Documents:**
- Receipt for application to replace a lost/stolen/damaged List A, B, or C document: valid for 90 days; employee must present the actual document within 90 days.
- Arrival portion of Form I-94/I-94A with a temporary I-551 stamp (LPR stamp): valid until expiration date on I-551 stamp or 1 year if no expiration date.
- Refugee travel document application receipt: valid for 90 days.

**Minors (Under Age 18) Lacking ID:**
If a minor (under 18) is unable to present a List B identity document, the employer may accept a school record or report card, clinic/doctor/hospital record, or day-care/nursery school record. A parent or legal guardian must complete Section 1 on behalf of the minor and attest that the minor is unable to present an identity document. The employer annotates Section 2: "Individual under age 18."

**Persons with Disabilities Lacking ID:**
If an individual with a disability (who is not an institutionalized individual) is unable to present a List B identity document, a parent, legal guardian, or representative of a social service agency may complete Section 1 and attest that the individual is unable to present an identity document. The employer records the representative's attestation in the Additional Information field.

## 5.0 EAD Auto-Extensions and the 540-Day Rule

### Employment Authorization Document (EAD) Auto-Extension
USCIS automatically extends the validity of certain EADs (Form I-766) when the employee:
1. Has filed a timely renewal application for the same category before the EAD expired.
2. Is in a category eligible for auto-extension.

**Auto-extension period:** Under a 2022 rule (effective May 4, 2022), USCIS extended the EAD auto-extension period from 180 days to **up to 540 days** from the EAD's expiration date. This applies to pending renewal applications filed on or after October 27, 2023.

**Note:** Employees whose EADs were auto-extended for 180 days under the prior rule and whose applications remained pending as of October 27, 2023, automatically received the benefit of the 540-day extension.

### Eligible EAD Categories for Auto-Extension
Not all EAD categories qualify. The auto-extension applies when the renewal application is for the same category and the category is listed on the auto-extension eligible list. Eligible categories include (but are not limited to):
- (a)(3) — Refugee
- (a)(4) — Parolee
- (a)(5) — Asylee
- (a)(7) — N-8 or N-9
- (a)(8) — Citizen of Micronesia, Marshall Islands, or Palau
- (a)(10) — Withholding of deportation/removal
- (a)(12) with a corresponding (c)(19) — TPS
- (c)(8) — Pending asylum application
- (c)(9) — Pending adjustment of status
- (c)(10) — Pending cancellation of removal
- (c)(16) — Creation of record
- (c)(20) — LIFE Act legalization
- (c)(22) — LIFE Act family unity
- (c)(24) — NACARA
- (c)(31) — VAWA

### How to Verify Auto-Extension Eligibility for I-9 Purposes
During reverification, the employer examines:
1. The employee's expired EAD showing a qualifying category code.
2. A USCIS receipt notice (Form I-797C) for the EAD renewal application showing the same qualifying category.

If both criteria are met, the employer:
- Records the receipt notice information in Section 2 (or Supplement B for reverification).
- Does not need to reverify until the auto-extension period ends (up to 540 days from EAD expiration) or until USCIS issues a decision on the renewal application, whichever comes first.
- Notes in Additional Information: "EAD auto-extension, receipt [number], expires [date 540 days after EAD expiration]."

### Employer Obligations During Auto-Extension
The employer must reverify when:
- USCIS denies the renewal EAD application (the employee's work authorization ends at denial), or
- The 540-day auto-extension period expires and no new EAD has been issued.

If the employee presents a new EAD before the auto-extension period expires, the employer should reverify using the new document.

## 6.0 Reverification — Supplement B

### What Is Reverification?
Reverification is the process of re-verifying an employee's employment authorization when their current work authorization expires. Reverification is completed in **Supplement B, Reverification and Rehire** (formerly Section 3).

### When Is Reverification Required?
Reverification is required when:
- An employee's employment authorization document with an expiration date expires.
- The employer rehires an employee within 3 years of the original I-9 completion date.

Reverification is **NOT** required for:
- U.S. citizens and nationals.
- Lawful permanent residents (LPRs) who presented a Permanent Resident Card (Form I-551) — LPR status does not expire even if the card does.
- Employees who presented a List B document for Section 2 (identity documents do not require reverification even if they expire).
- Employees whose employment authorization does not have an expiration date.

### Timing of Reverification
Employers should not reverify too early. Reverification must be completed **no later than the date the current employment authorization expires.** The employer should track expiration dates and reach out to employees before the expiration date. Employers may complete reverification up to 90 days before the work authorization expires.

### How to Complete Supplement B

**For reverification:**
1. Complete Block A — Reverification.
2. Enter the date of rehire (if applicable) or the date work authorization was reverified.
3. Record the new document information (title, number, expiration date) presented by the employee.
4. Have the authorized representative sign and date.

The employee presents a document showing continuing or new employment authorization — a List A or List C document (not List B identity-only documents).

### Rehires
An employer may use Supplement B to rehire an employee within 3 years of the original Section 1 completion date, rather than completing a new I-9. To complete for a rehire:
- Complete Block B — Rehire.
- Enter the rehire date.
- If the employee's work authorization has not expired, confirm the document previously recorded has not changed and no new document is needed.
- If the work authorization expired, reverify using Block A.

## 7.0 Special Categories of Employees

### Lawful Permanent Residents (LPRs / Green Card Holders)

LPRs may present:
- Form I-551 (Permanent Resident Card / Green Card) — List A document.
- Unexpired foreign passport with a temporary I-551 stamp or I-551 printed notation — List A document.

**Key rule:** LPR status does not expire even if the physical Green Card expires. An expired Green Card is NOT a valid I-9 document (it is not acceptable as a List A document), but an LPR is not required to be reverified — they may present any other acceptable document from List A, B, or C to show current employment authorization (e.g., a valid driver's license + unrestricted Social Security card).

Do not reverify LPRs who presented a Permanent Resident Card, even when the card expires.

### Refugees

Refugees may present:
- Form I-766 (EAD) — List A, valid for 1 year.
- Unexpired foreign passport with I-94 showing refugee admission — List A.
- List B identity document + List C document (unrestricted SSN card or original Social Security card showing "VALID FOR WORK ONLY WITH DHS AUTHORIZATION" is NOT acceptable; refugee SSN card is unrestricted).

Refugee status does not expire. Reverification is required only when the EAD (or other time-limited document) expires. After the initial EAD expires, the refugee may present any acceptable document from List A, B, or C.

### Asylees

Asylees (individuals granted asylum) have permanent employment authorization. They may present:
- EAD (Form I-766) issued for category (a)(5) — List A.
- I-94 or I-94A showing grant of asylum under INA § 208 — List A (with no expiration date or expiration date of "D/S" — the I-94 itself is the employment authorization document).
- Unrestricted Social Security card + List B identity document.

Reverification is required only when a time-limited EAD expires. If the asylee presents an I-94 showing asylum grant (no expiration date), no reverification is needed.

### Temporary Protected Status (TPS) Holders

TPS provides temporary employment authorization for nationals of designated countries experiencing ongoing armed conflict, environmental disasters, or other extraordinary conditions. TPS designations are subject to extension and termination by USCIS.

TPS holders may present:
- EAD with category (a)(12) — List A.
- Approval notice of TPS benefit (Form I-797) can serve in combination with an EAD in some cases.

**Auto-extension for TPS:** USCIS frequently issues Federal Register notices extending TPS EADs automatically. Employers should check USCIS's TPS page for the most current information on which designations are extended and for how long.

### F-1 Students and Optional Practical Training (OPT)

F-1 students may work under Optional Practical Training (OPT) authorized by USCIS. Types:
- **Pre-completion OPT** — work up to 20 hours/week while school is in session; full-time during breaks.
- **Post-completion OPT** — up to 12 months full-time after completing degree.
- **STEM OPT Extension** — additional 24 months for STEM graduates employed by E-Verify employers (Form I-983 required; student must report to DSO every 6 months).

For I-9 purposes, an F-1 on OPT presents:
- EAD (Form I-766) — List A document.

**F-1 Cap-Gap Extension:** F-1 students who are H-1B petitioned by an employer for the next fiscal year and whose OPT EAD would expire before H-1B status begins (October 1) are automatically extended until September 30 under the cap-gap rule. During cap-gap extension, the employer must notate the Additional Information field and may continue employing the student. For I-9 purposes: the expired EAD + I-20 with OPT authorization + cap-gap extension evidence (Form I-797 receipt notice for H-1B petition) supports continued employment.

### H-1B Specialty Occupation Workers

H-1B employees may work only for the H-1B petitioning employer (or a concurrent H-1B employer). Acceptable documents for I-9:
- Form I-94 showing H-1B admission + valid foreign passport — List A combination.
- EAD (if issued for H-1B-related category) — List A.

**H-1B portability:** An H-1B worker who has had an I-140 petition pending for 180 days may change jobs without their I-140 being revoked (under INA § 204(j) portability). This does not change the I-9 process — the employee still presents valid H-1B status documents.

**H-1B transfers:** When an H-1B employee moves to a new employer, the new employer files a new H-1B petition. Under H-1B portability rules, employment may begin as soon as the new employer files the petition (providing Form I-797 receipt notice). For I-9 at the new employer: the employee presents the I-94 from the prior H-1B status + foreign passport + the new employer's I-797 receipt notice.

### H-2A Agricultural Workers

H-2A workers are temporarily admitted to perform agricultural labor or services of a temporary or seasonal nature. Acceptable I-9 documents:
- Unexpired Form I-94 showing H-2A admission + valid foreign passport — List A.
- EAD (if issued).

H-2A status is tied to the specific petition. The expiration date is the I-94 expiration, not the passport expiration. Reverification is required when H-2A status expires.

### J-1 Exchange Visitors

J-1 exchange visitors may be authorized to work as part of their exchange program. The J-1 sponsor (program sponsor) issues a Form DS-2019 (Certificate of Eligibility). Acceptable I-9 documents:
- Unexpired Form I-94 with J-1 admission + valid foreign passport — List A.
- EAD (if issued for J-1-related employment category) — List A.

### TN Nonimmigrants (NAFTA/USMCA)

Canadian and Mexican professionals admitted under the USMCA (formerly NAFTA) TN category may work for a U.S. employer. Acceptable I-9 documents:
- Unexpired Canadian passport + Form I-94 with TN notation — List A (Canadians admitted at land border may not receive I-94; confirm with CBP).
- Unexpired Mexican passport + Form I-94 with TN notation — List A.

TN status can be renewed indefinitely, but there is no "portability" — the employee is tied to the sponsoring employer and position.

## 8.0 Continuing Employment — Employees Previously Verified

### Employees Hired Before November 7, 1986
No I-9 is required for workers continuously employed since before November 7, 1986.

### Re-Verifying Existing Employees with Expiring Work Authorization
If an employee's employment authorization document was recorded in Section 2 and that document has an expiration date, the employer must track the expiration date and reverify using Supplement B before or on the expiration date (not after). See Section 6.0 for how to complete reverification.

### What Triggers a New I-9 vs. Using Supplement B
- **Rehire within 3 years** — may use the existing I-9 plus Supplement B Block B.
- **Rehire after 3 years** — must complete a new Form I-9.
- **Change of employer name** — no new I-9 required; note the name change in the Additional Information field.
- **Employee changes name** — update the name in Section 1; no new I-9 required.

### E-Verify and Existing Employees
E-Verify is used for new hires (or for certain federal contractors required to run existing employees through E-Verify). An employer enrolled in E-Verify must create an E-Verify case for every new employee, regardless of citizenship status. Running existing employees through E-Verify outside of required circumstances is not permitted.

## 9.0 Correcting Form I-9 Errors

### Correcting Errors Found After Completion

**Employee Section 1 errors:**
- The employee (or their preparer/translator) must make corrections.
- Draw a single line through the incorrect information, enter the correct information, and initial and date the correction.
- Do not use correction fluid (white-out), do not obliterate incorrect information.

**Employer Section 2 / Supplement B errors:**
- The employer (or authorized representative) must make corrections.
- Draw a single line through the incorrect information, enter the correct information, and initial and date the correction.

### Correcting Missing Information
If information was simply omitted:
- Enter the missing information in the correct field.
- Initial and date the entry.

### Multiple Errors
If there are many errors, it may be cleaner to complete a new Form I-9 and attach it to the original. Attach a note explaining why a new form was completed.

### Backdating Is Prohibited
Dates must reflect when the action was actually completed, not the date the employee started or the ideal completion date. Never backdate I-9 forms.

### Documentation of Audit/Correction
When conducting a self-audit and making corrections, attach a cover memo explaining the audit and the nature of corrections. This shows good faith.

## 10.0 Record Retention

### How Long to Keep Form I-9

**For current employees:** Keep the I-9 for the duration of employment.

**After employment ends:** Keep for whichever is longer:
- **3 years** from the date of hire (date of Section 1 completion), OR
- **1 year** from the date employment ended.

**Formula:** Compare (hire date + 3 years) vs. (termination date + 1 year). Keep until the later of the two dates.

### Where to Store I-9 Forms
I-9 forms may be stored:
- On paper
- In a designated binder or electronic system
- At a location separate from personnel files (recommended but not required)

I-9 forms must be available for inspection by DHS, ICE, or DOL officers with 3 business days' advance notice.

### Electronic Storage of I-9 Forms

Employers may store I-9 forms electronically if the system:
1. Indexes, stores, and retrieves I-9 information accurately.
2. Produces a legible hard copy of the form and supporting documents (if retained).
3. Has an audit trail that records who accessed, created, corrected, or completed the form.
4. Has a quality assurance program/system with regular evaluations to ensure the system's integrity, accuracy, and reliability.
5. The employer has established controls to prevent unauthorized alteration.

**Electronic signature requirements:** Systems that capture electronic signatures must produce a printed attestation containing the information the signer reviewed, the date and time of the signature, and confirm that the signer read and understood what they were attesting to.

### Retention of Supporting Documents
Retaining copies of documents presented by the employee is optional unless:
- The employer participates in E-Verify (required for documents used to create the E-Verify case), or
- The employer uses the authorized alternative procedure (remote verification) — in that case, copies of all documents examined must be retained.

If an employer retains copies, they must retain copies for ALL employees, not selectively — selective document retention may constitute employment discrimination.

## 11.0 Anti-Discrimination Provisions

### The Law
INA § 274B, enforced by the DOJ Immigrant and Employee Rights Section (IER), prohibits:

1. **Citizenship or immigration status discrimination** — Unfair treatment in hiring, firing, or recruiting based on citizenship or immigration status, where the individual is a protected individual (U.S. citizen, national, LPR, refugee, or asylee). Applies to employers with 4 or more employees.

2. **National origin discrimination** — Unfair treatment based on national origin. Applies to employers with 4–14 employees (Title VII covers employers with 15+ employees for national origin discrimination).

3. **Document abuse** — Requesting more or different documents than required, rejecting documents that appear genuine and relate to the individual, or specifying which documents an employee must present. Applies to all employers regardless of size.

4. **Retaliation** — Intimidating, threatening, coercing, or retaliating against an individual who asserts their rights under INA § 274B.

### Common Prohibited Practices (Document Abuse)

- Asking employees of certain national origins or appearances to provide specific documents while not asking others.
- Requiring a green card when the employee has chosen to present a passport + I-94.
- Refusing to accept a document that reasonably appears genuine.
- Requiring more documents than needed (e.g., both a List B and List C when the employee has already presented a List A document).
- Asking to see additional proof of work authorization when there is no reasonable basis to doubt the document's authenticity.

### Acceptable Employer Practices
An employer MAY:
- Verify the authenticity of documents presented (examine, ask questions about information on the document).
- Require all employees (consistently) to present specific documents for the purpose of E-Verify (but only List B+C or a List A document, not specific types within those lists).
- Limit jobs to U.S. citizens when legally required by law, regulation, executive order, or government contract.

### Filing Complaints
Employees or job applicants who believe they have experienced I-9-related discrimination may file a charge with the IER within 180 days of the discriminatory act. Employers should consult legal counsel immediately upon receiving a discrimination charge.

## 12.0 Special Rules for Agricultural Recruiters and Referrers

### Who This Section Applies To
Agricultural associations, agricultural employers, and farm labor contractors who recruit or refer individuals for a fee for employment in the United States must also complete I-9 verification — they are treated as employers for I-9 purposes.

### H-2A Recruiters
Recruiters who recruit workers for H-2A agricultural positions must verify employment eligibility for the workers they recruit. The H-2A employer (not just the recruiter) is ultimately responsible for I-2 compliance.

### Farm Labor Contractors
Farm labor contractors licensed under the Migrant and Seasonal Agricultural Worker Protection Act (MSPA) must maintain I-9 compliance for workers they place. They are "employers" for I-9 purposes.

### Joint Employer Situations
When both an agricultural association and an employer are using the same workers, either may complete I-9 forms. They should agree in advance who will be responsible. The party that completes the I-9 retains the form and is responsible for its accuracy.

## 13.0 Acceptable Documents — Lists A, B, and C

The employee selects which document(s) to present. The employer may NOT require specific documents.

### List A — Documents Establishing Both Identity AND Employment Authorization

Employees present **one** List A document (no List B or C needed):

1. **U.S. Passport or U.S. Passport Card** — unrestricted, no expiration concerns for citizens.
2. **Permanent Resident Card (Form I-551 / Green Card)** — must be unexpired.
3. **Alien Registration Receipt Card (Form I-551)** — older version of Green Card, still valid if unexpired.
4. **Foreign passport with temporary I-551 stamp or I-551 printed notation** — for LPRs who have not yet received their Green Card.
5. **Employment Authorization Document (Form I-766 / EAD)** — must be unexpired (see Section 5.0 for auto-extensions).
6. **Foreign passport with I-94/I-94A showing nonimmigrant admission for employment** — for certain nonimmigrant visa holders (H-1B, L-1, TN, O-1, etc.); both the passport and the I-94 must be unexpired, and the I-94 must show employment-authorized status.
7. **Passport from Federated States of Micronesia (FSM) or Republic of the Marshall Islands (RMI) with Form I-94 or I-94A** — for citizens of these Compact of Free Association (COFA) nations.

### List B — Documents Establishing Identity Only

Employees present **one** List B document AND **one** List C document:

For employees **18 years or older:**
1. Driver's license or state ID card issued by a state or outlying possession of the U.S. — must have photo and identifying information (name, DOB, gender, height, eye color, address) or combination of listed features.
2. ID card issued by federal, state, or local government agencies — must have photo and identifying information.
3. School ID card with photograph.
4. Voter's registration card.
5. U.S. military card or draft record.
6. Military dependent's ID card.
7. U.S. Coast Guard Merchant Mariner Card.
8. Native American tribal document.
9. Driver's license issued by a Canadian government authority.

For persons **under age 18 who cannot present the above documents:**
10. School record or report card.
11. Clinic, doctor, or hospital record.
12. Day-care or nursery school record.

For persons who are **unable to present a photo ID** (certain disabilities):

A parent or legal guardian may complete Section 1 on behalf of the individual and attest in writing that the person is unable to present an identity document.

**Note on Real ID-compliant driver's licenses:** The employer should accept all driver's licenses that appear genuine. REAL ID compliance status does not affect I-9 acceptability.

### List C — Documents Establishing Employment Authorization Only

Employees present **one** List C document:

1. **U.S. Social Security Account Number Card** — must be unrestricted (cards bearing the legends "NOT VALID FOR EMPLOYMENT," "VALID FOR WORK ONLY WITH INS AUTHORIZATION," or "VALID FOR WORK ONLY WITH DHS AUTHORIZATION" are NOT acceptable for List C unless the employee qualifies based on their current employment authorization category). Unrestricted SSN cards have no such legend.
2. **Certification of report of birth abroad of a U.S. citizen (Form FS-545)** — issued by U.S. Department of State.
3. **Certification of birth abroad (Form DS-1350)** — issued by U.S. Department of State.
4. **U.S. Citizen ID Card (Form I-197)** — older document; still valid.
5. **ID card for use of resident citizen in the United States (Form I-179)** — older document; still valid.
6. **Employment authorization document issued by DHS** — any document issued by USCIS/DHS other than Form I-766 that is annotated as employment authorization (e.g., I-94 showing asylum grant with no expiration or expiration "D/S").
7. **Original or certified copy of birth certificate issued by a state, county, municipal authority, or outlying territory** bearing an official seal.
8. **Native American tribal document.**
9. **U.S. Citizen ID Card (Form I-197).**

## 14.0 Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

### Q: Can I use E-Verify in place of Form I-9?
No. E-Verify is an additional verification tool that supplements — not replaces — Form I-9. All employers must complete Form I-9. Employers enrolled in E-Verify must also complete I-9 and then run the employee through E-Verify within 3 business days of the employee's first day of work for pay.

### Q: Can I complete Section 2 before the employee's first day?
Yes, as long as the employee has already accepted the job offer. However, Section 1 must be completed by the first day of employment, and Section 2 must be completed within 3 business days of the first day of employment. You may review documents and complete Section 2 on or after the first day, up to 3 business days.

### Q: What if an employee presents a document that has expired?
If the document was expired at the time of initial verification (Section 2), it is not an acceptable document — do not record it. If the document expires after you verified it, you must reverify when the document expires (if it is an employment authorization document; not if it is a List B identity document). See Section 6.0.

### Q: Can I require an employee to present a specific document, such as a Green Card?
No. Document abuse (requiring specific documents) is a violation of INA § 274B. You may inform the employee of the three acceptable document options (List A, or List B + List C) and let them choose.

### Q: What happens if I discover an I-9 error during a self-audit?
Make corrections following the guidance in Section 9.0. Document the audit. Self-auditing and correcting errors in good faith is viewed positively by ICE during investigations.

### Q: Is a photocopy of a Green Card acceptable for Section 2?
No, unless you are using the authorized alternative procedure (remote examination) for E-Verify-enrolled employers. Otherwise, you must examine the original document. For E-Verify employers using the alternative procedure, you must retain a copy of the document.

### Q: How do I handle an employee who says their work authorization document is "pending"?
If the employee does not have a valid, unexpired employment authorization document, they cannot work until they can present acceptable I-9 documents. You may accept a receipt for a replacement document (Section 4.0), but not a pending application receipt, except in specific EAD auto-extension situations (Section 5.0).

### Q: Does an employee's I-9 transfer when they move to a related company?
Generally, no. If the employee is a new hire at the new legal entity, a new I-9 must be completed. An exception exists for corporate acquisitions where the new employer acquires the business and chooses to rely on the predecessor's I-9 forms — the new employer assumes responsibility for the I-9s and must ensure they are compliant.

### Q: What is the penalty for I-9 violations?
Civil fines for paperwork violations range from approximately $281 to $2,789 per violation (adjusted annually for inflation). Substantive violations (knowingly employing unauthorized workers) range significantly higher — up to $27,894+ per unauthorized employee for repeat violators. Criminal penalties are possible for pattern or practice violations.

### Q: When must I reverify a TPS employee?
When the TPS designation or the employee's EAD expires, you must reverify. However, USCIS frequently issues Federal Register notices automatically extending TPS designations and associated EADs. Check the USCIS TPS page and IER guidance for current auto-extensions before concluding that reverification is needed.

### Q: Can I accept a driver's license with a future issuance date?
The document must appear genuine and valid. A future issuance date would be unusual — if a document does not appear genuine, you should document your concern and discuss with legal counsel, but you should not reject the document solely on suspicion without a valid factual basis (to avoid potential discrimination).

### Q: What if an employee refuses to complete Section 1 by the first day?
An employee who does not complete Section 1 by the first day of employment may not work until the form is complete. If the employee refuses entirely, the employer may rescind the job offer without violating anti-discrimination laws (as long as the decision is not based on citizenship status or national origin).
